Best App Blockers for iPhone

Updated June 2026 · 6 min read

The short answer: the strongest dedicated app blockers for iPhone are Opal, Freedom, and Jomo — they lock chosen apps for scheduled periods. But pure blocking is easy to circumvent, so many people do better with a hybrid: a gentle pause by default that escalates the more you return, with hard blocking only when you switch it on. PauseMate does exactly that — a free, on-device, research-backed pause plus an optional Focus Mode — which is why it's worth a look before you commit to a subscription blocker.

Before comparing tools, it helps to be clear on the two jobs an "app blocker" can do. Hard blocking removes access entirely during set hours — you can't open the app at all. Friction keeps the app available but puts a pause, delay, or task in front of it, so opening becomes a deliberate choice. Blocking is the bigger hammer; friction is the gentler, more sustainable nudge. Knowing which you actually want makes the choice easy.

Why pure blocking sometimes backfires

Hard blockers are excellent for deep, scheduled focus. But on a bad day — stress, boredom, a late night — a determined brain finds the side door: delete and reinstall the app, open the web version in Safari, or simply tap "Ignore" on Apple's own limits. Apple Screen Time is especially easy to override because it was built for parents managing children, not adults managing themselves. The lesson isn't that blocking is useless; it's that the most durable setups make the choice conscious rather than relying on a wall you'll eventually climb.

What is the difference between an app blocker and a friction app?

An app blocker removes access during set hours — you simply cannot open the app. A friction app keeps it available but inserts a brief pause, delay, or task first, so opening becomes a conscious choice. Blocking is stronger but more restrictive; friction is gentler and tends to be more sustainable.

Is there a free app blocker for iPhone?

Do app blockers actually work?

They help, especially for deep work, but pure blocking can backfire — people delete the app, switch to a browser, or tap Ignore. Adding friction so the choice is conscious tends to last longer.
